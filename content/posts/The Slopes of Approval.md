## OK, so what should I do?

After the last chapter, I found myself asking a question I hadn't meant to ask yet.

If belief looks different once we stop treating it as a clean philosophical category and start asking what kind of neural state could produce it, what happens when we do the same thing to morality? Does neurology tell us anything useful about ethical judgment? Not whether an action is right or wrong. That would be much too fast. But does the substrate that produces moral judgment help dissolve some of the tensions that moral philosophy keeps running into, the way it seemed to help in epistemology?

That is the question I want to try here. The first two chapters built a way of seeing: concepts and beliefs as attractor valleys carved by learning, with continuous structure underneath discrete-feeling outputs. Once that picture was on the table, ethics became hard for me not to look at. Moral judgment has the same strange combination of certainty and gradation. It feels sharp from the inside, but it changes with context, history, attention, and training.

So I'm not trying to derive a new moral philosophy from brain science. I'm asking a narrower question: what does the machinery underneath moral judgment let us see more clearly?

## Why Sentiment?

Hume made a claim in 1739 that moral philosophy has been arguing about ever since: reason alone cannot move you. Sentiment moves you. By *sentiment*, I mean the felt pull of approval or disapproval, the emotional force that makes an action strike you as admirable, disgusting, cruel, noble, or wrong. When you witness cruelty and feel that it's wrong, you aren't concluding a syllogism. You're having something closer to a perceptual experience. The wrongness hits you before the reasoning starts.

Reason can tell you what's the case. It can tell you that an action will cause suffering, or that a particular lie will be discovered. It can't tell you to care. To get from "this action causes suffering" to "I should not do this," you need something other than reason, and Hume's name for that something is sentiment. Reason, in his famous phrase, is the slave of the passions.

Jonathan Haidt has a useful demonstration of this. He calls it moral dumbfounding.[^haidt-2001] He gives subjects a vignette about Julie and Mark, an adult brother and sister who, on vacation together, decide to have sex one time. They use two forms of contraception. They enjoy it. They agree never to do it again, and they never tell anyone. Subjects who read the vignette report strong moral disapproval. When asked why, they offer reasons. They could have a deformed child; but they used contraception. Someone might find out; but no one does. It might damage their relationship; but in the vignette it doesn't. Each reason collapses against the construction of the case. The judgment doesn't move with the reasons. People reach moral conclusions first and find reasons second; when the reasons fail, they find new reasons rather than changing the conclusion.

If that's roughly right, the work is happening before the reasoning catches up, and the reasoning isn't doing it. Whatever produces the sentiment is doing what matters. So look at the substrate.

Hume saw the question and tried to describe the machinery. In his account, sympathy was the process by which we share others' feelings, a theory of emotional contagion that anticipates modern work on mirror neurons and affective empathy. The general point of view was his name for the corrective: an imaginative exercise of judging from an impartial standpoint that keeps sympathy from being merely parochial. He distinguished natural virtues, which arise from sentiment directly, from artificial virtues, which need social scaffolding to take hold. These were the right kind of proposals. They were descriptions of how moral judgment actually works, not derivations of how logic says it should. But they pointed at a mechanism Hume couldn't see.

This has always struck me as right at the descriptive level. Watch someone encounter a moral violation, a betrayal, an act of cruelty to a child, a grotesque unfairness, and what you see is not a person running a calculation. You see a reaction: fast, visceral, automatic. The moral judgment arrives before the justification.

Our language is not proof of the psychology, but it is a useful trace of it. We call actions repulsive, filthy, sickening, chilling, cold-blooded. We talk about dirty money, tainted evidence, crushing guilt, and freezing someone out. Moral disapproval borrows the language of disgust, temperature, pressure, illness, and recoil. That does not settle the theory. It does suggest that moral response lives close to sensory and affective response.

I have to say up front that I'm taking Hume as a working assumption: moral judgments are, at root, expressions of sentiment rather than perceptions of mind-independent moral facts. That is where I stand.

But the realist alternative deserves more than a dismissal. A moral realist can say: yes, evolutionary and neural explanations may tell us why we have the moral intuitions we have. But that explains the *causes* of moral belief, not its *content* or *truth*. On that view, a substrate-level story about Hebbian carving, attractor dynamics, and basin geometry could be true, while some of the basins still track real moral features of the world. Enoch's robust realism is one version of this; Cuneo's parity arguments are another.

There is also a reverse worry, associated with Sharon Street: maybe evolutionary explanation debunks moral knowledge by showing that our basins were carved for fitness rather than truth. That is the live debate the realist has to engage with. I won't try to settle it here.

So treat what follows in two registers: as the working-out of a Humean assumption, and as a substrate-level account of moral psychology that several metaethical pictures could still use.


There's a second move I want to flag, because it's quieter than the first and easier to slide past. Even granting the Humean premise, I'm not trying to derive a moral philosophy from it. I'm asking what happens when we look at ethical questions through the substrate that produces sentiment. What does the machinery make visible? What confusions does it sharpen? What expectations does it make less plausible?

## So What Does the Neurology Tell Us?

The first thing to do is not to assume that sentiment is basin-shaped. It is to ask whether the mechanisms we have already been tracing show up again here.

### Is it really there in the wet biology?

Affective empathy runs through the anterior insula and parts of the cingulate, regions involved in bodily feeling, pain, conflict, and emotional salience.[^anterior-insula] In a 2004 study by Tania Singer, sixteen romantic couples came in for scanning. The woman lay in the scanner; her partner sat alongside, with both of their hands visible to her through a mirror. On some trials a small electric shock was delivered to the back of her hand. On other trials a cue indicated her partner's hand was being shocked instead. In both conditions her anterior insula and anterior cingulate lit up, and the activations overlapped.[^empathy-pain] Watching a loved one in pain ran through some of the same circuitry she used to feel pain herself.

The same circuitry handles moral reactions specifically, and it runs fast. Jean Decety and Stephanie Cacioppo recorded high-density EEG while subjects viewed images of intentional harm to others, alongside matched non-moral images. Source localization estimated amygdala-region activity at around 122 milliseconds and ventromedial prefrontal activity at around 182 milliseconds.[^moral-eeg] These timings should be read with one methodological caveat: source-localizing surface EEG to deep structures like the amygdala depends on inverse-modeling assumptions and is contested. With that caveat, the broad pattern in the literature is still important: moral evaluation runs through affective circuitry on a timescale shorter than deliberate analysis can plausibly cover. We do not need any one paper's exact depth estimate for that point.

### Do we learn to feel?

In Joseph LeDoux's work on fear conditioning, rats heard a tone paired repeatedly with a mild shock. After enough pairings, the tone alone produced freezing. Recordings from individual neurons in the basolateral amygdala showed that specific cells had rewritten their response to the tone: synaptic strengths between co-active cells had increased, and the change persisted.[^fear-conditioning] That isn't just analogous to Hebbian plasticity in cortex; it is Hebbian plasticity, in different circuitry. Affective associations get written into the affective circuitry by the same rule that writes conceptual associations into cortex.

### Are sentiments different from concepts?

The second piece concerns ventromedial prefrontal cortex and adjacent orbitofrontal regions. The names are a mouthful, but the role is familiar: these regions help evaluate options, track value, and connect feeling to choice. Value-based decisions appear to use the same broad architecture as the perceptual decisions chapter 2 looked at: graded accumulation, mutual inhibition between candidate options, and settling into a chosen state.

Three lines of evidence point this way.

The first is direct recording. Erin Rich and Jonathan Wallis trained monkeys to choose between two visual cues associated with different reward values, and recorded from neurons in orbitofrontal cortex while the animals decided. By decoding population activity moment by moment, they could see that the OFC representation didn't lock onto one option and stay there. It moved between the values of both options before settling. That is the kind of pattern you'd expect if competing populations were inhibiting one another and the system was gradually tipping toward a winner, not if a fixed value were being read off and acted on.[^rich-wallis] In miniature, it is the same shape we saw in the LIP recordings in chapter 2: graded competition resolving into commitment.

The second line is modeling work tied to human brain activity. Laurence Hunt and colleagues built attractor-network models of value-based choice and asked whether those models predicted the time course of brain signals while people made similar decisions. They did: MEG and fMRI signatures during choice tracked the competition-and-settling dynamics the model required. Easy choices settled fast. Hard choices settled slowly. That is what an attractor model with mutual inhibition would predict.[^hunt-2012]

The third line is causal perturbation. Work using transcranial direct-current stimulation over medial frontal cortex showed that perturbing the region produced behavioral shifts in value choices that an attractor-network model predicted in advance.[^vmpfc-attractor] The inference chain here is the longest of the three: model fits behavior; perturbation shifts behavior; shift fits the model. But it adds intervention to the more passive evidence from recording and observation.

No one of these three is decisive on its own. Together they converge. Direct recording shows competing-population dynamics in OFC during value choice. Modeling shows the dynamics match attractor predictions in human brains. Perturbation shows the system responds to intervention the way the model says it should. The substrate-parity claim, that value-based and perceptual decisions run on broadly the same architecture, hangs on the convergence rather than on any single anchor. The picture is well-supported but not airtight, and I want to flag that honestly.

And the wiring isn't isolated. The uncinate fasciculus connects the anterior temporal lobe, including the amygdala, with orbitofrontal and ventromedial prefrontal regions,[^uncinate] so sentiments and concepts seem to be reading and writing across one connected system.

### What about symmetry?

Emotions are often easier to turn on than off. A sudden ambiguous noise can throw the amygdala and defensive systems into high alert quickly, but the body does not return to baseline at the same speed. The threshold for activation is low; the threshold for reset is higher. Safety has to accumulate.

Aggression shows a related pattern. Work from David Anderson's lab on VMHvl circuits suggests that aggressive internal states can be represented by line-attractor-like dynamics.[^aggression-line-attractor] A line attractor is an attractor stretched along a range, so activity can ramp up, persist, and hold a state rather than simply snapping on and off. That matters here because aggression does not behave like a reflex that vanishes the moment the input disappears. Once an affective state has settled, leaving it can cost more than entering it did, and the path back does not retrace the path in.

Fear conditioning shows the same basic logic from another direction. Suppose a harmless sound is followed by something painful often enough. After a while, the sound itself starts to feel dangerous. The animal freezes before anything bad has happened, because the nervous system has learned the pairing. And when the fear fades, the original learning has not simply been erased. Extinction is closer to learning a second lesson: this sound is now safe here, in this context. That is why fear can return after time, stress, or a change of place.[^fear-conditioning] The system has history. It does not just read the present input and output the proportionate response.

So we find familiar mechanisms appearing again in affective circuitry: Hebbian plasticity in the amygdala, graded competition and settling in vmPFC, persistent affective states, and path-dependent transitions. The architecture of moral sentiment looks basin-like because the substrate has the same dynamical features we have already seen elsewhere.

What would have to be true for that to fail? The substrate would have to do something different from cortex: a non-Hebbian learning rule, or settling without mutual inhibition, or transitions without path-dependence. Some of that may yet turn up. So far, the evidence runs the other way.

That is enough for the claim I want here. Moral sentiment is not merely like a basin in some loose metaphorical sense. It appears to be built from basin-making machinery: learned associations, graded competition, settling, persistence, and path-dependence. From here the question changes shape. If moral sentiment is basin-shaped at the substrate, what does that geometry tell us about sentiment, and what does that in turn tell us about morality?

Some of it is already implicit in the architecture. Sentiment isn't a binary stamp on an action; it's a pull whose strength depends on how close the action sits to a valley floor. Approval and disapproval grade smoothly. The walls of the basins shift with context. Every witnessed act of kindness or cruelty, every reward or punishment absorbed, every story heard, every direct experience of harm or care, is a training example, and the landscape gets carved a little deeper or a little shallower each time. On the Humean picture I am using here, "wrong" is not a label being read off some moral object in the world. It is the gravity of a basin that learning has carved.

But that just tells us what moral feeling is. It doesn't tell us where the basins come from. That's the next question, and it has at least three different answers running on different timescales.

## What Carves the Basins

There's a bridge between Hebbian learning and natural selection worth naming, but briefly. The important point is not that natural selection and Hebbian learning are the same process. They are not. Hebbian learning changes synaptic weights within a lifetime. Natural selection changes inherited developmental machinery across generations.

But selection can still shape the basin-landscape indirectly. If a certain pattern of response helped organisms survive and reproduce, care for offspring, aversion to harm, sensitivity to betrayal, readiness for reciprocity, then organisms whose nervous systems more readily stabilized that response would tend to leave more descendants. Later generations would not inherit the learned judgment itself. They would inherit a nervous system biased toward forming that kind of judgment: a landscape in which some valleys are easier to carve, easier to enter, and harder to leave.

The basins don't take just any shape. Three carving processes act on the moral landscape, and they work on very different timescales.

The slowest is evolutionary. Across millions of years, selection pressure on social mammals has carved deep grooves into the affective machinery: basins for cooperation, for reciprocity, for harm-avoidance, for care of offspring. These grooves aren't outputs of human cognition. They're prerequisites for it. They were laid down before there were humans to feel them.

The clearest behavioral demonstration outside of humans is vampire bats. Wilkinson's field studies in the 1980s found that vampire bats regurgitate blood to colony-mates who haven't fed that night, and the recipients later return the favor preferentially to the bats that fed them.[^vampire-bats] That is tit-for-tat reciprocity in mammals, decades before anyone could read game theory papers about it. The selection pressure for reciprocity isn't a feature of human culture. It's a feature of being a long-lived social animal that has to survive many lean nights.

The same pressure shows up in the cross-cultural anthropology of human moral life. Cultures vary widely in their food taboos, sexual norms, and honor codes, but they converge on a small set of deep prohibitions: harming members of the in-group, abandoning one's children, betraying trusted partners.

The Ultimatum Game studies of Henrich and colleagues, run across fifteen small-scale societies, make the pattern concrete. In the game, one person proposes how to split a sum of money. The other person can accept the offer, or reject it so both players get nothing. A purely self-interested chooser should accept any nonzero offer. But no population matched that textbook prediction. The pattern of deviation was uneven. Some societies showed comparatively low rejection rates; others rejected even hyper-fair offers. But the *direction* of departure from pure self-interest, and the willingness to incur a personal cost to enforce some local conception of fairness, was widespread.[^ultimatum] What's universal isn't the precise threshold but the existence of fairness-related basins; their depth and shape vary.


The other two carving processes work fast enough to see in a single lifetime, and at the substrate level they work the same way: ordinary Hebbian learning, the kind we've been tracing since chapter 1, writing into whatever inputs the environment happens to supply.

Culture supplies one stream. Stories about who counts as a hero or a villain, laws about what gets punished, religious narratives about which acts the universe holds against you, the pattern of who gets honored at the dinner table and who doesn't: these are repeated, structured inputs. The basins they carve can become deep enough that, by adulthood, they feel less like inheritances than like discoveries about how the world is.

Slavery was, for centuries, morally invisible in many of the societies that practiced it. The basins for in-group cooperation were intact. The basins for out-group inclusion had been carved shallow, or carved against, by the cultural training the dominant group received. When those cultures changed, through testimony and contact with the people they had been excluding, what changed was the carving, not the underlying machinery.

Personal experience supplies the other stream, with the same architecture and the same level of effect. A single severe childhood injustice can carve a basin for unfairness deep enough to organize a lifetime's moral reactions around it. Knowing someone who has been seriously wronged often shifts moral judgments more than any argument about the wronged class can.

The shift in American attitudes toward gay marriage in the 1990s and 2000s tracked closely with whether respondents personally knew someone gay; the argument hadn't changed in the interim, but the training data had.[^contact-effect] Personal experience writes into the same circuitry that culture writes into, using the same rule, with no obvious priority either way.

That is what the layered picture looks like at full resolution. Evolution carves the deep grooves we share with the rest of our species. Culture carves on top of that, supplying the specific stories and norms a society repeats. Personal experience carves on top of that too, supplying what each particular life happens to encounter. The deep basins are stable across cultures because they were carved by selection pressures that operated on every human population. The shallower basins vary because the inputs vary. The same rule wrote them all.

If this is the geometry, what kind of moral picture does it leave us with? Not arbitrary, since the inputs are real and the constraints they impose are real. Not necessary in any logical sense either, since the basins depend on the human condition being roughly as it has been. The closest existing tradition is Aristotelian, and the version of it the geometry suggests is one I don't think Aristotle himself would have endorsed.

## The Cliffs of Moral Theory

This framework doesn't decide here whether utilitarianism or deontology is true. I won't make any normative claim. The point is structural. Both traditions often present moral judgment as if the decisive work happens somewhere cleaner than sentiment: in arithmetic, in logic, in universal law, in the consistent application of a rule. But the human endorsement of those verdicts still has to pass through the same machinery as everything else. We accept or resist the conclusion by how it lands in the landscape.

There is a further Humean pressure here. The decision to let arithmetic be the arbiter is not itself delivered by arithmetic. The decision to let universal law be the arbiter is not itself delivered by universal law. Somewhere upstream, we have to decide that impartial aggregation, consistency, rights, dignity, care, or some other value is the thing whose pull should organize the rest. That decision already belongs to sentiment. The theory may formalize the route, but sentiment chooses which route feels authoritative.

Both traditions tend to generate cliffs in the predictions they make about ordinary moral judgment. Utilitarianism generates them arithmetically: once the calculation comes out one way, the obligation falls sharply on that side. Five lives outweigh one. A distant stranger's suffering counts as much as the suffering of someone in front of me. A small sacrifice here can become mandatory if the aggregate benefit is large enough elsewhere.

Deontology generates cliffs logically: universalizable or not, permissible or forbidden, using a person merely as a means or not. One cliff is mathematical. The other is conceptual. Both predict that a graded, affective, history-sensitive system will behave as if moral space had clean edges. The substrate gives us reason to expect friction there. The more sophisticated versions, rule consequentialism, scalar consequentialism, two-level utilitarianism, threshold deontology, exist partly *because* the simple versions over-predict edge behavior. Their additions are responses to exactly the substrate-fit problem this chapter is naming.

That is why the cliffs never remain purely theoretical. We judge the utilitarian conclusion by whether the sacrifice it demands feels noble, monstrous, compulsory, or insane. We judge the deontological conclusion by whether the rule feels principled, rigid, evasive, or sacred. The theory may offer the form of the answer, but sentiment supplies the gravity that makes the answer feel acceptable or intolerable. The substrate is local, vivid, partial, and training-sensitive. It cares more easily about the child in front of us than the statistical stranger far away. It treats physical contact, intention, proximity, and social meaning as morally weighty, even when a theory wants to abstract them away.

Kant's project makes the logical version especially clear. In one sense it was the opposite of Hume's, and the irony is that Hume made it possible. Kant famously credited Hume with awakening him from his "dogmatic slumber," and then spent the rest of his career building a system designed to put reason back in charge. Where Hume grounded morality in feeling, Kant grounded it in the *categorical imperative*: act only according to a principle you could will to be a universal law. Morality, for Kant, isn't about how you feel. It's about what reason demands. A moral rule must be universalizable, non-contradictory, and applicable regardless of your sentiments about it.

Kant, in other words, tried to impose a cliff. And the pattern should look familiar by now. It's the same structural move we've seen twice before. Analytic philosophy demanded necessary and sufficient conditions for concepts that have sloped boundaries. Epistemology demanded binary belief from a system that operates in continuous credences. Moral theory often demands sharp obligation from a system that runs on graded, overlapping, context-sensitive sentiments. The tools assume edges. The material doesn't.

The result, I think, is the same: persistent paradoxes at the boundaries.

Consider the trolley problem, or rather, the family of trolley problems, because what makes them interesting is the pattern across the variations. A runaway trolley will kill five people. You can divert it to a side track where it will kill one. Most people say: divert. Now change the scenario. You're on a bridge. You can push a large man onto the tracks, stopping the trolley but killing him. Same arithmetic, one death to save five. But most people say: don't push. And they can't explain why.

The deontological framework has to find a principled distinction. "Don't use a person as a means" handles the bridge case but creates problems elsewhere. Is diverting the trolley also using the one person as a means? Is quarantining someone during a pandemic? Every proposed principle either draws the line in the wrong place for some variant or generates new contradictions when extended. Decades of moral philosophy have produced increasingly elaborate rule systems to handle an expanding set of trolley variants, and none has achieved consensus.

The attractor framework suggests a different diagnosis. "Do not kill" and "save lives" are two deep basins. The trolley problem plants the marble on the ridge between them. It's a case specifically engineered to sit at the saddle point where the pull from both basins is roughly equal, the moral version of the saddle points we saw when a category could tip either way.

The divert case puts the marble slightly closer to "save lives": the killing is indirect, impersonal, a side effect. The push case puts the marble slightly closer to "do not kill": the killing is direct, physical, personal. The agonizing quality of the dilemma *is* the experience of the marble sitting on a saddle point. And the reason there's no consensus isn't that we haven't found the right principle. It's that different people's basins have slightly different shapes, carved by different moral experiences, so the saddle sits in a different place for each person.

## Aristotle, Re-Read

There's a tradition in moral philosophy that came close to the attractor picture without having the formal tools to express it. I have to say I'm surprised to find this, but it fits the framework better than I expected.

Aristotle's account of how a person becomes virtuous is short and concrete. We become builders by building, lyre-players by playing the lyre, just by doing just acts, brave by doing brave acts.[^nic-eth-ii] Virtue, in his framework, is a *hexis*: a settled disposition acquired through repeated action, eventually becoming the place a person naturally ends up. You don't reach courage by learning a principle about courage. You reach it by practicing courageous behavior until courage is where you settle.

That is, almost word for word, a description of what Hebbian learning does to a basin. Repeated co-activation strengthens the synapses linking the relevant cells, and with enough repetition the basin gets deep enough that the response becomes the default, the valley floor the system settles into without deliberation. Aristotle didn't have the mathematics for it, but he had the phenomenology exactly right. Virtue is trained stability.

The interesting question is where Aristotle's normative force comes from, because hexis on its own can't supply it. You can ask: why aim for the courageous basin rather than the cowardly one? Aristotle's answer isn't a rule (Kant) or a calculation (Mill). It's a claim about what kind of creature we are.

Humans have a *telos*, an end or function that defines what flourishing looks like. The virtues are the character states that let a human being fulfill that nature. The *ought* is grounded in the *is* of what kind of creature you happen to be. A knife ought to be sharp because cutting is what knives are for. A human ought to be courageous, just, and temperate because reason and social life are what humans are for.

Now, Aristotle thought the telos was metaphysically fixed, part of the eternal essence of the species. We don't need to follow him there. But let's allow a reading that takes the telos and replaces it with evolved human nature. Notice what happens if you replace "metaphysical essence" with "deep evolutionary attractors." Some of the structure of the argument survives, and some of it does not, and I want to be honest about which is which.

What survives: humans have a nature; that nature is one source of the deep moral basins; and the basins for reciprocity, harm-avoidance, care for offspring, and in-group fairness aren't arbitrary cultural products but consequences of the kind of organism we are.

What does *not* survive intact is the keystone Aristotle's argument was originally built on: that the telos grounds an *ought*. Aristotelian telos is teleological, meaning it is about what humans are *for* in a normative sense. Evolution has no teleology in that sense. It has outcomes, not purposes. So the substitution preserves Aristotle's architecture but loses his foundation. The "kind of creature we are" can describe the basins; it can't, by itself, license the move from "we are this kind of creature" to "we ought to live the way this kind of creature flourishes." That is a genuinely missing piece, not a translation.

But I need to be careful here, because evolution didn't carve only the basins we'd want to call virtuous. The same selection pressures that produced reciprocity also produced in-group favoritism. The same pressures that produced care for offspring produced aggression toward perceived threats. Dominance hierarchies, xenophobia, status competition: these are deep evolutionary basins too, carved by pressures just as ancient and just as robust. The attractor framework explains the *shape* of all of them equally well. It doesn't pick favorites. If you ask "why should I aim for the reciprocity basin rather than the dominance basin?" the geometry has no answer. The valleys are just valleys. The gravity is just gravity.

This means the Hume-Aristotle synthesis closes one loop but leaves another open. Hume told us that sentiment, not reason, creates moral gravity. Aristotle told us that human nature is the source of those sentiments. Put them together through evolutionary biology and the descriptive pieces fit.

The sentiments Hume observed aren't free-floating feelings that could have gone any direction. They're the felt experience of navigating a landscape that evolution carved, the landscape that Aristotle's intuition about human nature was pointing toward. Hume was right about what produces the gravity: sentiment. Aristotle was right about where the landscape comes from: what kind of creature we are. Neither had the descriptive bridge that connects them. The attractor framework, read through evolutionary biology, may supply that bridge.

What this synthesis doesn't supply is a reason to prefer some basins over others. The geometry can describe reciprocity and xenophobia with equal accuracy and equal indifference. A basin's depth doesn't make it good. Its evolutionary age doesn't make it right. If you want to argue that cooperation, care, and reciprocity are better than aggression and dominance, and I do, you need grounds the geometry itself can't provide. The framework explains why we have the moral sentiments we have. Which sentiments to cultivate is a question the physics can't settle.

Two more pieces of Aristotle's system fit the picture and are worth naming. His *phronesis* (practical wisdom, the trained sensitivity to what a particular situation calls for) is, in geometric terms, gradient-reading: learned attunement to the local shape of the landscape, picked up by the same experiential process that carved the basins to begin with. Phronesis doesn't tell you which valleys to aim for; the telos answers that, upstream. Phronesis is what you do once you're inside the landscape.

And the "thick" moral concepts that Philippa Foot and Elizabeth Anscombe wrote about, words like *cruel*, *courageous*, and *generous* (terms that are descriptive and evaluative at once), are what you'd expect basins to look like from inside the language used to talk about them.[^foot-anscombe] Cruelty isn't a neutral description that then gets a moral label attached to it. It's a single structure where recognizing the act and condemning it are one event. That's why moral reactions are fast, and why attempts to separate the descriptive content of moral terms from their evaluative content always feel forced.

The virtue tradition had the right picture of what moral judgment feels like from the inside, and the right instinct about where the normative grounding would have to come from: not from rules imposed on human nature, but from human nature itself. What it lacked was a formal account of why trained dispositions have the specific structure they do, and a non-metaphysical version of why human nature is stable enough to matter philosophically. The attractor geometry can help with the first. Evolutionary biology can help with the second. The picture that comes out is Aristotelian without being Aristotle's.

## Yes, This Is a Form of Relativism

A natural worry runs alongside everything I've been saying. If moral basins are carved by inputs that vary, hasn't the framework just relabeled relativism? On this picture there's no ground to say one culture's morality is better than another's. Slavery was wrong for us because our basins say so; for a culture whose basins endorsed it, it wasn't.

I want to accept the form of this worry without conceding the substance. Yes, on this picture, morality depends on the human condition: on evolutionary inheritance, on social structures, on the specific landscape that biology, culture, and personal experience have carved. But that doesn't mean anything goes.

Reciprocity isn't a coin flip; it's a convergent solution to the repeated social games any sufficiently social species ends up playing. Harm-avoidance isn't optional; organisms that don't avoid harm don't persist. The deep basins converge across cultures because they were carved by pressures that operate on every human population. The shallow basins vary because the inputs vary. That's just the carving picture from earlier in the chapter.

A caveat on the shallow/deep distinction, which has been doing heavy lifting all along: I haven't given a precise criterion for when a basin counts as "deep." Cross-cultural convergence is one indicator. So are steepness of walls, resistance to counter-evidence, breadth of catchment area. But I don't have an operational definition that ties these together cleanly, and I'm using the depth metaphor as a working instrument. Specifying it more rigorously is downstream work, not something this chapter delivers.

One more thing this concession doesn't do. It doesn't collapse the is-ought distinction. G. E. Moore made the point in 1903 that whatever natural property you point to, pleasure, fitness, social utility, or the pull of an attractor basin, someone can always ask: "But is that good?" The question stays open.

A deep valley of disapproval toward cruelty is a fact about the landscape. It tells you the system will reliably produce disapproval when it encounters cruelty. It doesn't tell you that the system *should* produce that response, or that cruelty is really wrong in some mind-independent sense. The gravity is the *is*. The *ought* is a different question, and the geometry can't answer it.

## What the Framework Predicts

A picture that only retrofits to what's already known is a story rather than a framework. If this one is right, it should predict things that don't fall out of competing accounts of moral judgment. Four such predictions emerge from the chapter so far, and the last is the one I would most like to see tested directly.

**Moral dilemmas cluster at saddle points between strong sentiments.** They aren't randomly distributed across the moral domain. The dilemmas that produce the most disagreement and the most distress are the cases where two deep basins compete, where the marble sits on a ridge with strong gravity on both sides.

The trolley cases are the clean example. "Do not kill" and "save lives" are two deep basins, and each version of the problem plants the marble on the ridge between them. The divert case sits slightly closer to "save lives" because the killing is indirect, a side effect of saving the five. The push case sits slightly closer to "do not kill" because the killing is direct and personal, a hand on the man's back. The agonizing quality of the dilemma is the experience of the marble sitting on a saddle point. There's no consensus answer because different people's basins have slightly different shapes, so the saddle sits in a different place for each person.

Other cases sit on similar ridges: autonomy versus harm prevention in arguments over euthanasia or drug policy, fairness versus loyalty in whistleblowing, individual rights versus collective welfare in quarantine and taxation. The claim isn't just that these are hard cases. Their difficulty is a property of the geometry, the flatness of the ridge, and easy moral cases are easy because the marble sits deep in a single basin with no competing pull.

**Moral certainty correlates with basin depth, not with propositional justification.** By propositional justification, I mean reasons you can state as premises and conclusions. The moral judgments people hold most firmly are often the ones they can articulate least well. The wrongness of harming children, the incest taboo, the gut-level reaction to betrayal: these are deep, steep-walled basins carved by evolutionary pressure, and people are maximally certain about them despite being unable to give a satisfying propositional defense.

Conversely, the moral questions people are *least* certain about, optimal tax policy, the ethics of gene editing, obligations to future generations, are the ones they can reason about most fluently. If moral judgment were proposition-driven, the correlation should go the other way. You should be most certain about the things you can argue for best. The attractor picture predicts the reverse, and the reverse is what we observe.

**Moral persuasion works through landscape-reshaping, not through derivation.** What changes people's moral judgments are narratives, direct experience, empathy exercises, exposure to suffering they had been ignoring, meeting someone from a group they had been abstractly judging. These work because they supply new training data that reshapes the basin.

Syllogistic argument rarely works by itself. Peter Singer's case for extreme altruism is the standing example: the premises are clear, the conclusion follows, the argument satisfies every Kantian criterion for a valid moral argument,[^singer-1972] and very few people, even among those who find the argument forceful, live as if they accept it.[^charitable-giving] The geometric reason is that there's a deep, evolutionarily ancient "care for kin and near others" basin and a shallower, more recent "care for distant strangers" basin, and Singer's argument tries to move the marble between them using logic alone. It fails because logical validity describes the landscape but can't reshape it. Reason navigates the terrain; it doesn't create the gravity.

A note on what this does and doesn't claim. The framework explains why Singer's argument often fails to move people. That's a claim about moral psychology, not about whether the argument is correct. Singer might be right that distance is morally irrelevant; the geometry can't adjudicate that.

**Hysteresis in moral judgment.** Chapter 1 established the general signature: once a network settles into a basin, the path out is not the path in. Chapter 2 saw it in trust dynamics. We saw it earlier in this chapter in the morphed-faces emotion-recognition data. The framework predicts the same signature should appear in moral judgment proper, and the most specific version of that prediction is the one I would most like to see tested directly.

Take an ambiguous moral case that can be framed as a harm case or as a fairness case. Present it to one group with the harm framing first, then gradually change the details until the case looks more like fairness. Present the same case to another group with the fairness framing first, then gradually change it toward harm. If moral categories were binary, the switching threshold should be the same in both directions. If they're attractor basins, the path-dependence will show up.

## Where This Leaves Us

We started with a working assumption: that moral judgments are at root sentiment, not perception of mind-independent moral facts. From there we asked what looking at the substrate of sentiment tells us about morality. The substrate turns out to use the same machinery the previous chapters were tracing: Hebbian learning, competing populations, settling into basins. The moral landscape that comes out of it looks remarkably like what Aristotle described, minus the metaphysical commitments he wanted to give it.

If something like this picture is right, then moral life looks the way the substrate carves it: stable where the basins are deep, variable where the basins vary, training-sensitive throughout. That's a description, not a defense.

Whether moral life *is* "real" in some stronger metaphysical sense, whether the basins track moral facts or merely produce moral judgments, is a question this chapter has set aside, not settled. What the geometry does say is that the arguments people give for their moral positions aren't, in general, what's holding the positions in place. The basins are. The arguments come up to meet them. That's a claim about the psychology of moral conviction, and it's compatible with several different metaphysical pictures of what conviction is conviction *of*.

None of this delivers an ought. A deep basin is still just a deep basin, and the question "but is that good?" stays open. What the geometry does is name what kind of thing moral judgment is, so that argument and reform about it can address the actual structure rather than the structure morality was supposed to have under a different picture of what's underneath.

The hysteresis prediction is what I would most like to see tested directly. If it runs the way the geometry says it should, this picture earns standing as more than a story. If it doesn't, the picture has to change. Either way, the geometry is something you can run experiments against.

We've been navigating these basins all our lives. It's worth knowing what they are.
