*When Will Humans Achieve AMI?*

*A Philosophical Dialogue Between Two Primates*

Darwin and Wallace sat in their favorite fig tree, watching the strange hairless creatures below.

"I'm telling you," Wallace said, "they're getting close to AMI. That one yesterday climbed almost three meters."

Darwin snorted. "Three meters? My nephew does five before breakfast. And it had to look where to put its hands. No instinct. That's not AMI."

The humans huddled around a small fire, making peculiar vocalizations.

"The noise-making is getting sophisticated," Wallace admitted. "Almost like communication."

"Almost," Darwin emphasized. "But can they communicate dominance with a single glance? Warn about predators with the right shriek? They need whole sequences of sounds just to say 'danger.' The AMI threshold requires true social intelligence."

Years passed. The humans scratched markings into clay tablets.

"Persistent symbols," Wallace noted. "Interesting."

"Can they distinguish good mushrooms from poison ones by smell?" Darwin countered. "Any juvenile knows that. Instinctive foraging knowledge is a core AMI requirement."

The humans built structures - huts, then buildings, then cities with aqueducts.

"The coordination required," Wallace began.

"They're compensating for deficiency," Darwin interrupted. "They can't navigate the canopy, so they build. AMI requires natural three-dimensional forest movement."

The humans developed mathematics, literature, medicine, universities.

"They've split the atom," Wallace noted.

"Can they tell when the dominant male is in a bad mood by grooming patterns?" Darwin asked. "Manipulating quantum matter is impressive, but they still can't read a room."

"They went to the moon, Darwin."

"And yet they can't distinguish edible bark from inedible bark without tools. Interesting tricks, definitely some form of intelligence, but not Artificial Monkey Intelligence. It's something else. Something alien."

Wallace watched the humans work on their latest incomprehensible project.

"Maybe," she said quietly, "we're asking the wrong question."
